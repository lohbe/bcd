I am using **two** behavioural-insight (BI) techniques, selected from Michie et al.'s
Behaviour Change Technique (BCT) taxonomy v1 (Michie et al., 2013). They are complementary:
one creates the action, the other provides the feedback loop. The **EAST** framework
(Behavioural Insights Team, 2014) is applied as a cross-cutting *design lens* in Part 3
rather than a standalone technique.

## Technique 1 — Implementation intentions + habit stacking  (BCT 1.4, Action planning)

<!-- ~200 words -->

**What it is & how I will use it.**
Implementation intentions are explicit "If [situation], then I will [action]" plans that
link an environmental cue to a goal-directed response (Gollwitzer, 1999). I combine this
with habit stacking (Clear, 2018; consistent with Lally et al., 2010): the new behaviour is
anchored to an existing automatic cue — my morning coffee. My plan:
*“After I pour my coffee at 08:00, I will open the editor and start a 25-minute sprint.”*
The cue, time, and action are all pre-specified.

**Why it is suitable.**
My target behaviour is *time- and cue-bound* (8–10am), precisely the condition under which
implementation intentions are most effective. Gollwitzer & Sheeran's (2006) meta-analysis
of 94 studies reports a medium-to-large effect (d ≈ 0.65) across health, academic, and
pro-social behaviours. Lally et al. (2010) show that habit automaticity accrues with
repetition at a consistent context, and Wood & Rünger (2016) emphasise that stable cues
drive context–response links. Because my window is narrow and recurring, an if–then plan
directly targets the failure mode (drifting past 10am) rather than relying on willpower.

## Technique 2 — Self-monitoring  (BCT 2.3)

<!-- ~200 words -->

**What it is & how I will use it.**
Self-monitoring is the deliberate observation and recording of one's own behaviour
(Michie et al., 2013). I use two channels: (1) an **objective** measure — the first git
commit timestamp of each day, auto-captured by `scripts/first_commits.py` — and (2) a
**structured reflective** diary (`diary/log.md` + `data/daily-log.csv`) logging cue,
barriers, facilitators, emotion (1–5), and a short reflection.

**Why it is suitable.**
Self-monitoring is among the most reliably effective BCTs. Kanfer & Gaelick (1986) frame
self-monitoring as the foundational stage of self-regulation, and Korotitsch & Nelson-Gray
(1999) review it as a robust standalone intervention. Michie et al. (2011) found
self-monitoring to be the BCT most consistently associated with successful behaviour
change. Crucially, the commit timestamp removes self-report bias from the *timing* measure,
while the diary supplies the qualitative data needed to revise the plan (e.g. recurring
barriers). Bandura's self-regulation work (Bandura, 1991; Zimmerman, 2000) predicts that
combining an externalised record with reflective judgement strengthens self-efficacy —
directly relevant to sustaining a fledgling coding habit.
